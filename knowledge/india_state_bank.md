# India Data Centre Opportunity Knowledge Bank for State Targeting

Checked: 2026-07-08  
Purpose: state targeting, state-pitch generation, and model-ready policy/context bank for Indian data-centre opportunities.

## Executive Judgment

Building a state-by-state policy and execution knowledge bank is strategically strong. The competitive gap is no longer just "which state has a data-centre policy." The useful question is which state combines policy depth, electricity economics, land and approval machinery, labor and essential-service treatment, water and climate feasibility, connectivity, and actual project execution.

The clearest policy-plus-execution leaders are Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh, Telangana, Tamil Nadu, and Andhra Pradesh. Odisha is the strongest under-exploited policy regime with less visible hyperscale execution. Gujarat is now too serious to treat as a minor comparator because its official incentive portal confirms greenfield data-centre incentives and the state has a large Dholera AI-ready campus MoU. West Bengal is now clause-verifiable through the state ITE/Sahayata portal and official policy PDF. Karnataka is stronger than the earlier draft suggested because the state has an official Data Centre Policy 2022-27 page and operational guidelines, though the current 2026 opportunity is still mostly a distributed AI/data-zone and coastal-spoke story. Haryana is now clause-verifiable through the OCR text of the Haryana Gazette notification for the New Haryana Data Centre Policy 2026 and should be treated as a serious NCR follow-up state rather than a secondary-news-only lead.

There is a meaningful difference between states with standalone data-centre policies and states that embed data centres inside broader IT/ITeS or emerging-tech frameworks. Tamil Nadu, Uttar Pradesh, Telangana, Odisha, West Bengal, Karnataka, and Haryana have standalone data-centre policy foundations. Maharashtra has a serious data-centre chapter inside its IT/ITeS Policy 2023. Gujarat handles data centres through the IT/ITeS Policy 2022-27 and the DST incentive portal. Haryana's 2026 policy is now OCR-readable from the Gazette PDF, so its incentive structure can be modeled at clause level, subject to final legal review of the original Gazette PDF.

For an India-first but GCC-linked strategy, the best framing is not just "bring Gulf capital into India." The sharper framing is: GCC capital, energy relationships, cooling expertise, sovereign-infrastructure investors, and hyperscale customers can be matched with Indian states offering dual-grid power, open-access renewable power, stamp-duty and electricity-duty relief, cable/subsea advantages, and fast-track approvals. The most relevant states for this GCC-to-India pitch are Maharashtra, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, Gujarat, Tamil Nadu, and Uttar Pradesh.

- West Bengal wins if the thesis is eastern gateway plus government-controlled land aggregation around New Town/Bengal Silicon Valley.
- Karnataka wins if the thesis is distributed AI/data zones and a future Mangaluru coastal spoke, not classic Bengaluru hyperscale.
- Haryana wins if the thesis is NCR/Gurugram-Manesar demand plus a notified 2026 policy, essential-service continuity, high FAR, dual-grid intent, time-bound approvals, and employment-linked incentives.
- Himachal Pradesh and Uttarakhand are edge/DR/public-sector hosting niches, not first-priority hyperscale destinations on current evidence.

## Essential-Service and Public-Utility Status

Essential-service treatment deserves its own diligence field because it is one of the most valuable non-fiscal benefits a data-centre policy can offer in India. Data centres are 24x7x365 critical digital infrastructure; if a state explicitly treats them as essential services, public utilities, or continuous industries, the investor case improves on operating continuity, labor-dispute resilience, crisis permissions, inspection treatment, power-cut exemptions, and SLA bankability. It does not remove ordinary legal compliance, guarantee unlimited power, or override project-level permits, but it materially reduces the risk that a campus is treated like a normal commercial real-estate asset during shutdowns, strikes, or administrative restrictions.

## First-Wave State Targets

### Maharashtra

Maharashtra should remain a first-wave state. It combines the strongest operating ecosystem with one of the most detailed policy frameworks. Its IT/ITeS Policy 2023 gives data centres infrastructure status, essential-service status under ESMA, continuous 24x7x365 power supply, renewable open access, industrial tariff treatment, and support for data centres in any zone, including residential, no-development, and green zones. The policy also provides 24x7 water supply support in industrial-area contexts and uses the MAHITI portal as the single-window mechanism.

The hard economics are strong. The policy provides 100% stamp-duty exemption for land and premises transactions and related instruments, permanent electricity-duty exemption for registered data-centre units, industrial tariff power supply, and an INR 1/unit power tariff subsidy for five years for new data-centre units outside Zone I. MIDC-led data-centre park development is contemplated with pre-clearance and support for substation/transmission infrastructure.

Maharashtra also has unusually useful enforcement language. If a private IT park or data-centre park diverts reserved built-up area to non-IT/non-DC use, the policy imposes a daily penalty tied to ready-reckoner value, requires restoration to original use, and escalates repeated misuse under the MRTP Act. This is not proof of broad contract enforcement across all matters, but it is a concrete signal that land-use misuse in technology parks is not meant to be consequence-free.

Execution proof is also strong. Reuters reported AWS would invest about USD 8.2 billion in Maharashtra by 2029-30. Maharashtra has also seen major logistics, data-centre, and GCC park announcements around Navi Mumbai. The best pitch is: Mumbai/Navi Mumbai is India's safest execution-first data-centre market where policy detail, subsea/interconnect relevance, financial depth, and operator demand outweigh pure subsidy maximization.

Best-fit niches:

- Hyperscale and colocation around Mumbai/Navi Mumbai.
- Financial-services, cloud, AI, and enterprise workloads.
- GCC-linked capital seeking the most bankable Indian execution base.
- Power, cooling, and substation vendors attached to a mature cluster.

Red flags:

- Land cost.
- Power and water pressure.
- Mumbai concentration and climate/flood resilience.
- Zone I may not receive the same tariff subsidy benefits as other regions.

### Uttar Pradesh

Uttar Pradesh is one of the strongest first-wave targets. Its official 2021 policy page is unusually clear. The policy targeted 250 MW of data-centre industry, INR 20,000 crore investment, and at least three private data-centre parks. It includes 7% capital subsidy on fixed capital investment excluding land and building, park-level interest subsidy, land subsidy by region, 100% stamp-duty exemption on first transaction and 50% on second transaction, 100% electricity-duty exemption for 10 years, dual-grid support for the first three data-centre parks, and transmission/wheeling concessions.

UP also has an official government-order trail for declaring data-centre industries an essential service. That matters for operating continuity and should be captured as a separate field in the knowledge bank rather than buried in a policy summary.

The 2026 direction is highly material but not yet fully clause-safe. Reporting and an Invest UP-indexed press clipping show the UP cabinet approved Data Centre Policy 2026 on 6 July 2026. ETGovernment reports that it replaces the 2021 policy, targets more than INR 2 lakh crore and more than 2 GW of additional data-centre capacity, creates differentiated incentives for data-centre parks, standalone units, and edge data centres, adds special incentives for AI compute infrastructure, supports green-certified data centres, and allows customized packages for projects above 500 MW. The user-supplied GovJobs current-affairs text corroborates the same cabinet-approval direction, AI/GPU focus, Bundelkhand/Purvanchal special-incentive direction, 7,500 long-term and 50,000 short-term jobs claim, and older-policy execution claim of about INR 21,343 crore approved, 644 MW capacity, and seven operational projects by 2026. Chronicle India separately confirms cabinet approval of the Startup Policy 2026 and Data Centre Policy 2026 on 6 July 2026, but its accessible article text focuses mostly on startup-policy incentives rather than data-centre clauses. These are useful corroborating sources, but neither is an official government order or policy text. Until the official government order/policy PDF is pulled, treat 2026 as cabinet-approved and directionally detailed, but not safe for exact incentive calculations.

Execution proof is strong and improving. Government statements have described eight data-centre parks across Noida, Greater Noida, Yamuna Expressway, Ghaziabad, Lucknow, Kanpur, Varanasi, and Agra, with proposals or interest from HCL, Adani, NTT, Yotta, Sterlite, and Sify. Yotta's Greater Noida AI/hyperscale footprint makes the state especially relevant for AI infrastructure and NCR enterprise demand.

UP also has a useful enforcement example: Noida Authority reportedly refused an Adani group sublease request before project completion, relying on lease conditions and occupancy/functionality requirements. This gives the state a rare "post-allotment discipline" data point. It is not a universal rule, but it is useful for diligence and pitch sophistication.

Best-fit niches:

- NCR-adjacent hyperscale and AI-ready parks.
- Enterprise, government, cloud, and low-latency North India workloads.
- Investors seeking aggressive land/power/capex incentives.
- Multi-city cluster strategy beyond Noida.

Red flags:

- 2026 policy has strong reporting and Invest UP-indexed press evidence, but still needs the official policy/government order before clause-level use.
- Some execution evidence remains announcement-heavy.
- Land authority terms can be strict; benefit assumptions must match lease terms.

### Telangana

Telangana is a first-wave target because it is one of the most operator-friendly policy regimes. The Telangana Data Centres Policy routes building permissions through TS-iPASS, commits to dual power-grid networks, permits renewable energy through open access up to one-third of requirements, supports captive solar/wind approvals, and states that eligible data centres can receive power at the cost of generation.

The policy also classifies data-centre firms as essential services because they serve global customers on a 24x7x365 basis. It provides exemptions from statutory power cuts and certain inspections, land at subsidized cost as decided by the competent committee, up to 50% rebate on building fees, startup/SME lease-rental support, R&D grants, internet-cost reimbursement, and patent-filing reimbursement. A distinctive feature is the government's stated intention to assure 10% of business to players with presence in the Data Centres Campus.

Execution proof is strong. Microsoft has been reported as bringing its largest India data-centre site in Hyderabad online around mid-2026. AWS has a strategic framework for multi-year cloud data-centre infrastructure expansion in Telangana. UPC Volt's proposed INR 5,000 crore, 100 MW AI-ready data-centre campus at Bharat Future City reinforces the AI-ready narrative.

Best-fit niches:

- AI-ready inland cloud and enterprise workloads.
- GCC and software demand linked to Hyderabad's talent ecosystem.
- Operators who value business continuity and power treatment.
- Data-centre-adjacent suppliers in power, racks, cooling, and controls.

Red flags:

- Policy is from 2016 and should be checked for current operative status or superseding changes.
- Some incentive details are committee-decided, so not all benefits are calculator-ready.
- Water and grid constraints should still be assessed project by project.

### Tamil Nadu

Tamil Nadu is still a first-wave state because Chennai is a proven data-centre and cable-linked southern gateway. However, the most important caveat is timing: the official Tamil Nadu page says the Data Centre Policy 2021 applies to data centres set up after 1 April 2021 and is applicable until 31 March 2026. Since this report is checked on 8 July 2026, the first diligence task is confirming whether the policy has been extended, replaced, or allowed to lapse.

The official page confirms industrial tariff electricity, green energy/green certification, multi-level generator stacking, stamp-duty and fiscal incentives on land/building, dual power, higher boundary walls, rooftop chillers without FSI inclusion, no zoning restrictions, no floor-to-ceiling restrictions, lower car-parking/window requirements, training incentives, and subsidy to MSME units providing services to data centres.

The deeper policy material and secondary comparison indicate granular district-category incentives, including Category C land and stamp-duty advantages, MSME service ecosystem support, and training subsidies. The 2025-26 Industries, Investment Promotion and Commerce Department policy-note excerpt supplied by the user does not show a new data-centre policy or legislative update. It does, however, strengthen the land-bank and infrastructure story: SIPCOT has created and managed 50 industrial parks across 48,926.48 acres in 24 districts, facilitated 3,390 industrial units, attracted INR 1.99 lakh crore of investment, and generated 8.79 lakh jobs. It also confirms that plots in SIPCOT Industrial Parks receive upfront 50% subsidy in Category C districts and 10% back-ended subsidy in Category A and B districts under the Tamil Nadu Industrial Policy 2021 and other sectoral policies. These are useful for site-selection and state-pitch modeling, but should not be treated as a fresh data-centre policy extension.

The same policy-note excerpt is useful on execution and infrastructure adjacency. Siruseri is described as transforming from an IT cluster into a data-centre hub with NPCI, Sify Infinit Spaces, Equinix India, Nxtra Data, and STT Global Data Centre. It also records sustainable industrial-water initiatives, including 45 MLD TTRO capacity at Koyambedu supplying about 15 MLD tertiary treated water to five SIPCOT parks, a 20 MLD TTRO plant at Kelavarapalli for Hosur/Shoolagiri, and a 60 MLD desalination plant under development at Mullakkadu/Thoothukudi. This helps the Tamil Nadu pitch move beyond old incentives into a more practical cluster, water, and industrial-land story.

The latest public reporting found in this pass says Tamil Nadu is allowing the 2021 policy to lapse and is recalibrating away from broad data-centre tax breaks because of water, power, environmental-cost, and low direct-employment concerns. The model should therefore treat Tamil Nadu as a proven Chennai/interconnect execution state but not assume old incentives are live after 31 March 2026.

Execution proof remains strong. Chennai has Equinix, NTT, STT, CtrlS, Sify, Nxtra, and other major operator presence. STT GDC India announced on 19 February 2026 the launch of its fourth Chennai data centre at Siruseri, with the Siruseri campus designed as a 45 MW AI-ready facility, 7.2 MW operational in first phase, 40 MW already operational at Ambattur, approximately 130 MW total Chennai development potential including existing operational capacity, and a strategic MoU with the Government of Tamil Nadu proposing INR 4,200 crore of AI-ready data-centre infrastructure investment. Recent reporting around L&T investment in Tamil Nadu including electronics manufacturing and a data centre further strengthens the supply-chain/data-centre adjacency angle. The state also has a sophisticated industrial base and cable/interconnection logic.

Best-fit niches:

- Chennai as southern interconnect and cable-rich data-centre hub.
- AI/data-centre supply chain and electronics manufacturing adjacency.
- DR/backup nodes in lower-cost districts if current policy still supports them.
- GCC-linked operators needing southern India coverage.

Red flags:

- Policy validity after 31 March 2026 must be resolved.
- Public reporting indicates the state is letting the 2021 policy lapse and may prefer more selective/location-based support.
- Chennai has water and flood-risk concerns.
- State commentary in 2026 suggests some recalibration away from broad data-centre incentive competition.

### Andhra Pradesh

Andhra Pradesh should be elevated from comparator to first-wave competitive threat. The state is actively pulling giga-scale AI/data-centre investments that affect the targeting logic for Karnataka, Telangana, Tamil Nadu, and even Maharashtra.

The biggest execution signal is Google's USD 15 billion AI data-centre hub in Visakhapatnam with Adani and Airtel ecosystem participation. AP also has Digital Connexion's reported USD 11 billion plan for 1 GW of AI data capacity in Visakhapatnam, plus Tillman Global's reported 300 MW hyperscale campus MoU. These are not small signals; they change India's hyperscale map.

AP's policy innovation is also now clause-safe for the power-distribution piece. The official Energy Department G.O.Ms.No.32 dated 22 April 2026 approves a policy framework to recommend grant of Deemed Distribution License status to eligible strategic data-centre projects. Eligibility is a single developer or investor with minimum connected load of 300 MW or more within Andhra Pradesh, and the investor can aggregate connected load across multiple data-centre locations in the state to meet the threshold.

The DDL framework allows development, ownership, operation, and maintenance of internal electricity distribution infrastructure within the notified project boundary, treated as the "minimum area" for the license route. Supply is restricted to data-centre loads within the licensed area; third-party supply outside the APERC-approved license area is not allowed. The DDL can procure power from lawful sources including renewable bilateral PPAs, open access, captive solar/wind/hybrid projects with BESS, and power exchanges. It must obtain LTOA/MTOA connectivity where needed, bear transmission charges, register with SLDC as a scheduled entity, follow scheduling/deviation rules, and maintain metering/telemetry and compliance reporting. The G.O. requires at least 51% of total energy requirement from renewable energy. DISCOM safeguards are important: DDL status applies to new greenfield data centres already being developed or planned, existing DISCOM consumers cannot simply migrate, standby supply is chargeable under APERC rules, exit requires six months' notice and transition planning, and APERC can revoke DDL status for violations.

Best-fit niches:

- Vizag as AI/hyperscale coastal mega-campus.
- Subsea gateway and port-linked infrastructure.
- Renewable-energy-backed giga-scale campuses.
- GCC capital looking for very large AI infrastructure bets.

Red flags:

- The DDL framework is official, but it solves power architecture rather than providing a complete data-centre incentive code.
- It does not itself classify data centres as essential services.
- Water stress and social-license risk are material, especially around mega AI data centres.
- Subsidy intensity may not be replicable for all investors.

## Strong but Selective Opportunities

### Odisha

Odisha has one of the most generous official policy regimes on paper and deserves a serious second-track pursuit. The official Invest Odisha page confirms concessional land through IDCO, 100% stamp-duty exemption on first transaction and 50% on second transaction, 100% reimbursement of land-conversion charges, building-fee subsidy, 20% capital subsidy on fixed capital investment excluding land capped at INR 25 crore per unit, 100% electricity-duty and electrical-inspection-fee exemption for 10 years up to 5 MVA, 30% power-bill subsidy for five years capped at INR 2 crore, energy-audit reimbursement, 100% transmission/wheeling reimbursement for captive renewable power set up in the state, 100% net SGST reimbursement for seven years up to eligible FCI, skilling support, EPF/ESI reimbursement, bandwidth subsidy, and green-production incentives.

For data-centre parks, Odisha adds concessional land, 100% stamp-duty exemption on first transaction, 100% land-conversion reimbursement, 100% electricity-duty reimbursement for five years, 25% infrastructure-development subsidy capped at INR 10 crore, and 5% interest subsidy for seven years capped at INR 25 crore. The first two anchor units can also receive data-centre unit incentives.

The operating regime is also strong. Odisha provides single-window clearance, online application and tracking, an apex committee, OCAC as nodal implementation/monitoring agency, public-utility treatment under the Industrial Disputes Act framework, specified labor-law exemptions, and general permission for three-shift operations with women at night subject to safety self-certification.

Odisha's weakness is not policy; it is visible private hyperscale execution density. That makes it a white-space/first-mover state rather than the safest execution state.

Best-fit niches:

- First-mover data-centre park development.
- Green/renewable-backed DC campuses.
- Bhubaneswar/Khurda/Cuttack digital infrastructure.
- Eastern India and mineral/industrial corridor adjacency.

Red flags:

- Less visible private hyperscale campus proof than Maharashtra, Telangana, Tamil Nadu, UP, or AP.
- Some incentives are capped at levels that may matter less for giga-scale hyperscale.
- Connectivity and customer-demand depth need project-level diligence.

### Gujarat

Gujarat should be upgraded in the knowledge bank. The official DST Incentive Management Portal confirms a dedicated greenfield data-centre incentive path under the IT/ITeS Policy 2022-27. It lists up to 25% eligible capex support subject to a maximum ceiling of INR 150 crore for greenfield data centres and an INR 1/unit power tariff subsidy for five years. It also confirms cable landing station incentives: up to 25% capex support capped at INR 20 crore and INR 1/unit power tariff subsidy for five years.

This matters because Gujarat is not only an industrial state; it is positioning around AI infrastructure, Dholera, GIFT City, and coastal/cable infrastructure. The L&T Vyom MoU for a 250 MW green AI-ready campus at Dholera, reportedly involving INR 25,000 crore, makes Gujarat a serious west-coast challenger.

Best-fit niches:

- Dholera/GIFT-linked AI-ready data-centre campus.
- Greenfield hyperscale with structured capex support.
- Cable landing station and digital infrastructure adjacency.
- GCC capital seeking west-coast alternatives to Mumbai.

Red flags:

- Need official Dholera MoU/government release for clause-safe project details.
- Gujarat policy sits inside IT/ITeS framework, not a standalone data-centre policy.
- Demand depth versus Mumbai/Chennai/Hyderabad/NCR should be tested.

### West Bengal

West Bengal is useful as an eastern gateway and cluster play, and it is now verified through official state sources rather than only secondary comparison. The Sahayata portal of the Department of Information Technology & Electronics publishes the Data Centre Policy 2021 page with downloads for the policy, notification, advisory group order, and State Level Empowered Committee order. The official policy PDF is government-hosted and notified as No. 490-Estt/ITE-18011/2/2021 dated 06.09.2021.

The official policy gives West Bengal a 400 MW data-centre capacity target by 2025, an INR 20,000 crore investment target, and a goal of three dedicated state-of-the-art data-centre parks. It is valid for five years from notification or until replaced. Policy clauses classify data-centre organisations under Essential Services, route single-window clearances through Shilpa Sathi, appoint WBEIDC/Webel as nodal agency, create a PIU under MD Webel, and establish a State Level Empowered Committee chaired by the Chief Secretary.

The clause-level incentive story is now safe to use. West Bengal offers 100% stamp-duty and registration-fee exemption for transactions related to setting up data centres, electricity-duty waiver for five years from commercial operation, industrial treatment of electricity use irrespective of location, dual power-grid networks, support for captive power approvals through single window, 24x7 power and internet facilitation, support for fibre connectivity, 24x7 high-speed water facilitation, captive water-treatment support, right of way for optical fibre under the West Bengal Broadband Policy 2020, 3.0 + 1.0 purchasable FAR, exclusion of basement parking/storage/DG space from FAR, up to 60% ground coverage, self-certification-based inspection relief, preference in public procurement of cloud storage, edge data-centre promotion in government IT/industrial parks, and three-shift operations with women at night subject to safety self-certification.

Execution is more interesting than subsidy. Reporting says Bengal Silicon Valley in New Town had 41 companies allotted plots, 23 under construction, and NTT, STT, and CtrlS data centres nearing launch. It also reported significant land subsidy from approximately INR 40 crore per acre market value to INR 5 crore per acre, with the government not considering further subsidy at the time. This means the best pitch is not "maximum cash incentives"; it is "eastern gateway digital cluster with government-controlled land aggregation and visible operator pipeline."

Best-fit niches:

- Kolkata/New Town as eastern India data-centre cluster.
- DR/eastern gateway for Bangladesh/Northeast/eastern enterprise demand.
- Government-backed land aggregation.

Red flags:

- Policy was notified on 06.09.2021 for five years, so it runs to September 2026 unless replaced earlier; a replacement/extension check is needed before late-2026 use.
- Incentive expansion may be limited if the state is not considering further subsidy.
- Execution pipeline should be separated from operating-campus proof.

### Karnataka

Karnataka is stronger than the earlier draft suggested because the Government of Karnataka has an official Data Centre Policy 2022-2027 page and operational guidelines. The policy remains in force for five years from 7 May 2022 or until a new policy is announced. The official page frames Karnataka as a South India data-centre market with a 15% share in data-centre capacity, eight-plus planned data centres, and INR 10,000 crore planned investment within five years.

The investor-facing incentive stack is clear for projects outside Bengaluru Urban district. It includes 100% electricity-duty exemption for five years from commercial operation, industrial power tariff for data centres with at least 30% green energy in total power consumption, green power tariff reimbursement of INR 0.50/unit for five years for data centres using more than 50% renewable energy, 100% exemption of land-conversion fees outside Bengaluru Urban district, concessional registration charges, 100% stamp-duty exemption up to 10 acres outside Bengaluru, 10% land subsidy capped at INR 3 crore outside Bengaluru Urban district, and one-time 7% capital subsidy on fixed assets excluding land and building, capped at INR 10 crore, outside Bengaluru Urban district.

Even with this official policy stack, Karnataka is not currently the cleanest immediate subsidy pitch versus Maharashtra, UP, Odisha, or Gujarat. The better 2026 framing is future distributed AI/data-zone strategy. Bengaluru has deep demand, talent, and cloud ecosystem, but state officials have acknowledged water and power constraints for hyperscale growth. Recent reporting says the state is planning AI data-centre zones across the state, including Dakshina Kannada, and data parks totaling 1,000 MW across Bengaluru, Mysuru, and Mangaluru. Reporting also indicates the state is reassessing policy around liquid cooling, treated water, coastal locations, and sustainability.

The most interesting Karnataka opportunity is Mangaluru. KDEM, Silicon Beach Programme, and Deloitte feasibility work positions Mangaluru as a cost-efficient coastal data-centre candidate, with land and power advantages, a possible 1 GW+ sustainable AI-ready cluster, and a hub-and-spoke architecture with Bengaluru as the core and Mangaluru as the spoke.

Best-fit niches:

- Mangaluru coastal AI-ready spoke.
- Bengaluru enterprise and AI demand linked to distributed infrastructure.
- Treated-water/liquid-cooling/sustainable DC innovation.
- Subsea cable advocacy and coastal digital corridor development.

Red flags:

- Incentives are strongest outside Bengaluru Urban district; Bengaluru hyperscale expansion is constrained by water and power.
- Mangaluru is still feasibility-led rather than fully executed.

### Haryana

Haryana is now more interesting than older market maps suggest. The Haryana Data Centre Policy 2026 has been verified at gazette-entry level: Haryana Gazette Extra-Ordinary No. 81-2026/Ext, dated 27 May 2026, Department of Industries & Commerce, subject "New Haryana Data Centre Policy 2026", notification number 20/12/2026-4IB-1. The user-supplied OCR text makes the policy clause-usable. It is valid for five years from notification or until a new policy/amendment, whichever is earlier, and targets INR 10,000 crore cumulative investment during the policy period.

Eligibility is clear. Data-centre units require at least 1 MW contracted power capacity, while data-centre parks require at least 20 MW cumulative contracted power capacity dedicated to data-centre operations. Hyperscale data centres are defined as above 10 MW, core data centres as more than 1 MW and up to 10 MW, and edge data centres as 300 kW up to 1 MW. Captive-use data-centre units are excluded from incentives under this policy. Units commencing operations after 7 July 2022 and until notification of the 2026 policy remain eligible to claim benefits under the 2022 policy.

The fiscal package is now clause-verifiable. Hyper data-centre units receive 20% eligible CAPEX reimbursement capped at INR 25 crore per unit. Core data centres receive 10% eligible CAPEX reimbursement capped at INR 1 crore per MW. Edge data-centre reimbursement is pushed to the Haryana Progressive MSME and Exports Policy, 2026. CAPEX disbursement is in ten annual instalments after commencement of operations or achievement of committed eligible capital investment. Eligible CAPEX includes building/infrastructure, utilities, power backup, machinery, equipment, hardware/software, new captive renewable projects of at least 100 kW, effluent/waste systems, recognized R&D facilities, stamp duty, external development charges, cooling technologies, and green-building certification expenses.

The OPEX and employment package is unusually useful for modeling. Hyper data centres receive 50% eligible OPEX reimbursement for five years capped at INR 8 crore per annum; core data centres receive 30% reimbursement capped at INR 4 crore per annum. Eligible OPEX includes net SGST, wheeling/transmission charges paid to Haryana government entities for importing energy from outside the state, electricity duty, and globally recognized data-centre certification expenses. Local employment subsidies run for 10 years and are tied to the proportion of Haryana local employees. There is a higher subsidy category for women, SC, Divyang, Agniveer, and ex-servicemen employees, and HKRN recruitment can trigger 100% reimbursement of employer and employee EPF statutory contribution for five years, subject to caps.

For data-centre parks, developers can receive 20% eligible CAPEX support capped at INR 15 crore, disbursed against development/operation milestones, plus 5% interest subsidy on term loans for five years capped at INR 5 crore per annum. Ultra-mega and mega projects are also eligible for customized packages through the Haryana Enterprise Promotion Board based on the state's cost-benefit analysis model.

The non-fiscal package is where Haryana becomes strategically sharp. The policy records relaxed FAR of 500%, 60% ground coverage, and unrestricted maximum permissible height under the Haryana Building Code; separate infrastructure-industry status for covered data centres through notification no. 02/13/2023-41B-II dated 22 November 2023; dual-grid quality-power intent; proposed energy-intensive-industry declaration; 24x7 water-supply intent; redundant last-mile high-speed connectivity; RoW through the Single Roof Clearance System; HARTRON empanelment and first-preference public-procurement treatment for operational Haryana data centres; HSIIDC plot reservation on need basis; pre-commencement approvals within 10 working days; commencement approvals within 15 working days; property tax at industrial rates; interest on delayed incentives; international partnership facilitation; and permission for data centres in Transport and Communication Zones. Most importantly for continuity, the 2026 policy confirms that covered data centres have been declared essential services under the Haryana Essential Services Maintenance Act, 1974 through notification no. 47/1/2017-5 dated 12 July 2023.

The strategic angle is obvious: Gurugram-Manesar-Faridabad proximity to NCR enterprise demand, GCC offices, government demand, and major corporate customers. The state has also reportedly signed an Anant Raj MoU for INR 20,000 crore in data-centre infrastructure.

Best-fit niches:

- NCR enterprise/GCC demand.
- Manesar/Gurugram data-centre and cloud-services expansion.
- High-FAR dense campus development if policy details verify.

Red flags:

- OCR is now usable, but final legal review should still be against the original Gazette PDF.
- Captive-use data centres are excluded from incentives; this matters for enterprise self-use campuses.
- Edge data-centre incentives are deferred to the Haryana Progressive MSME and Exports Policy, 2026 rather than fully specified here.
- MoU size has reporting variation; confirm company filing or state release.
- Power and land economics need clause-level validation.

## How to Turn This into an Asia Group-Grade Pitch System

The knowledge bank should not remain a static memo. It should become a state pitch engine. Every pitch should answer three questions:

1. What exact clause or mechanism makes this state investable?
2. What operating problem does that clause solve: power cost, building approval, labor continuity, land economics, water/cooling, or connectivity?
3. What real project or enforcement event proves the state can execute or enforce?

Weak pitch: "UP has attractive data-centre incentives."  
Strong pitch: "UP combines 10-year electricity-duty exemption, land/stamp-duty support, dual-grid provisions for early parks, essential-service treatment, and a live NCR/Greater Noida execution base; the 2026 policy may expand this to a 2 GW-plus AI-ready cluster, pending gazette verification."

Weak pitch: "Tamil Nadu is investor-friendly."  
Strong pitch: "Tamil Nadu's official 2021 policy engineered data-centre-specific building and power facilitation around Chennai's cable/interconnect advantage, but the policy ran to 31 March 2026, so the next diligence question is whether the state renews, narrows, or replaces incentives."

Weak pitch: "Maharashtra is India's biggest data-centre market."  
Strong pitch: "Maharashtra pairs Mumbai/Navi Mumbai's operator and cable ecosystem with 2023 policy detail: infrastructure status, ESMA treatment, open access, permanent electricity-duty exemption, stamp-duty relief, zoning flexibility, and explicit misuse penalties."

## Open Questions and Limitations

Some 2026 developments are too new to treat as settled clause-by-clause law. UP Data Centre Policy 2026 should be used as cabinet-approved directional intelligence until the official government order/policy PDF is obtained. Andhra Pradesh's DDL framework is now official through Energy Department G.O.Ms.No.32 dated 22.04.2026, but that document is a power-distribution framework rather than a full incentive policy. Haryana Data Centre Policy 2026 is now clause-readable through OCR, but final legal review should still compare the OCR against the original Gazette PDF. West Bengal 2021 is now official-policy verified, but any 2026 AI data-centre budget/incentive direction still needs separate official budget evidence.

West Bengal's official policy PDF and notification are now directly identified. The remaining West Bengal issue is policy horizon: it was notified on 06.09.2021 for five years or until replacement, so confirm replacement/extension before relying on it after September 2026.

Tamil Nadu's policy content is strong, but its official validity ran to 31 March 2026. Treat it as historically proven and potentially renewable, not automatically active.

Contract enforcement credibility is harder to prove than policy generosity. UP has a useful sublease/lease-discipline example, Maharashtra has codified misuse penalties, Tamil Nadu has deemed-approval/time-bound facilitation architecture, Telangana has TS-iPASS and essential-service treatment, Haryana has a clause-readable 2026 policy with essential-service treatment and time-bound approval commitments, and Odisha has GO SWIFT/OCAC single-window architecture. For other states, execution evidence is currently stronger than enforcement evidence.
